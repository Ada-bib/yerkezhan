About Violence & Abuse

Violence and abuse can affect anyone regardless of age, gender, race, or economic status. It takes many forms including physical, emotional, sexual, financial, and digital abuse. Understanding the signs and having resources can help prevent abuse and support those affected.

This website provides information and resources for everyone. Whether you're seeking help for yourself, supporting someone else, or simply want to learn more about these issues, you've come to the right place.

Remember: You are not alone, and help is available.

Types of Abuse

Physical abuse involves the use of physical force against someone in a way that injures or endangers that person. Examples include hitting, kicking, burning, choking, or any other harmful physical contact.

Warning Signs: Unexplained injuries, frequent injuries, fearfulness, withdrawal, wearing clothes to hide injuries even in warm weather.

Emotional abuse involves behaviors that harm another person mentally or emotionally. This can include verbal aggression, intimidation, manipulation, humiliation, isolation, and controlling behaviors.

Warning Signs: Low self-esteem, excessive fear, anxiety or depression, decreased socialization, personality changes.

Sexual abuse is unwanted sexual activity, with perpetrators using force, making threats, or taking advantage of individuals not able to give consent.

Warning Signs: Fear of specific people or places, sleep disturbances, self-harm, withdrawal, inappropriate sexual knowledge or behavior.

Financial abuse involves controlling a person's ability to acquire, use, or maintain financial resources. This can include preventing someone from working, stealing money, or racking up debt in their name.

Warning Signs: Unexplained withdrawals from accounts, missing money or valuables, unpaid bills, sudden changes in financial documents.

Digital abuse uses technology and the internet to bully, harass, stalk, or intimidate. This can include unwanted texting, hacking accounts, tracking locations, or spreading damaging content online.

Warning Signs: Partner demands passwords, controls social media use, sends threatening messages, uses technology to monitor activities.

Hotlines & Help Resources

These confidential hotlines provide 24/7 support, information, and resources for anyone affected by violence or abuse. All calls are confidential and many offer services in multiple languages.

National Domestic Violence Hotline

1-800-799-SAFE (7233)

24/7 support, crisis intervention, safety planning, and referrals to local services.

RAINN (Sexual Assault)

1-800-656-HOPE (4673)

Connects callers to local sexual assault service providers with trained staff members.

National Child Abuse Hotline

1-800-4-A-CHILD (422-4453)

Professional crisis counselors for children, parents, and concerned individuals.

Crisis Text Line

Text HOME to 741741

Free 24/7 support via text message for those in crisis.

National Suicide Prevention Lifeline

988 or 1-800-273-TALK (8255)

Support for people in distress and those around them.

StrongHearts Native Helpline

1-844-762-8483

Domestic violence and dating violence helpline for Native Americans.

Self-Defense Techniques

Learning basic self-defense can increase confidence and provide tools to protect yourself in threatening situations. Remember: the goal of self-defense is to get away safely, not to engage in a fight.

Disclaimer: These techniques are for educational purposes only. Taking a formal self-defense class with qualified instructors is recommended.

Proper Stance

Stand with feet shoulder-width apart, knees slightly bent, hands raised to protect your face, one foot slightly in front of the other for balance.

This stance lowers your center of gravity, making you harder to knock over and ready to move quickly in any direction.

Wrist Release

If someone grabs your wrist, quickly rotate your arm toward their thumb (the weakest part of their grip), while simultaneously pulling your arm away.

Practice this motion to develop muscle memory so it becomes instinctive when needed.

Vulnerable Points

If you need to defend yourself, target vulnerable areas: eyes, nose, throat, groin, and knees. Using the heel of your palm to strike upward at the nose or using your knee can be effective.

Remember, the goal is to create an opportunity to escape, not to continue fighting.

Use Your Voice

Yell "NO!" or "BACK OFF!" in a commanding voice. This can startle an attacker and attract attention from others who might help.

Practice projecting your voice from your diaphragm rather than your throat for maximum volume and impact.

Safety Planning

Creating a safety plan is crucial for those at risk of violence:

  • Know the safest rooms in your home (rooms with exits and phones)
  • Have emergency contacts saved under code names
  • Keep important documents, some money, and necessities packed
  • Share your safety concerns with trusted friends or neighbors
  • Practice exit routes and safety plans regularly

Creating a Personal Safety Plan

A safety plan is a personalized, practical plan that includes ways to remain safe while in a relationship, planning to leave, or after you leave.

If you are still in an abusive relationship:

  • Identify safe areas of your home where there are no weapons and there are ways to escape. If arguments occur, try to move to those areas.
  • Keep a phone accessible at all times and know what numbers to call for help.
  • Let trusted friends and neighbors know about your situation and develop a visual or verbal signal to notify them when you need help.
  • Practice how to get out safely. Practice with your children if applicable.
  • Plan for what you will do if your abuser finds out about your plan.
  • Keep weapons like guns and knives locked away and as inaccessible as possible.

If you are planning to leave:

  • Create a "go bag" with important items you'll need when leaving.
  • Hide essential items with friends or family so they're not discovered.
  • Open a savings account in your own name.
  • Leave money, an extra set of keys, copies of important documents, extra medicines, and clothes with someone you trust.
  • Determine who would be able to let you stay with them or lend you money.
  • If possible, open a post office box or use a safe address for receiving mail.

Documents to take with you:

  • Identification for yourself and your children
  • Birth certificates, social security cards, insurance cards
  • Medical records
  • School records
  • Money, bankbooks, credit cards, ATM cards
  • Legal papers: protective order, lease or home deed, car registration

Warning Signs of Abuse

Recognizing the warning signs of abuse is the first step toward getting help. Be aware that abusers often begin with subtle behaviors that escalate over time.

Early Warning Signs:

  • Extreme jealousy or possessiveness
  • Controlling behavior (checking your phone, monitoring where you go)
  • Isolating you from friends and family
  • Moving too quickly in the relationship
  • Blaming others for their problems or feelings
  • Hypersensitivity to small slights
  • Cruelty to animals or children
  • Using "playful" force during sex
  • Pressuring you to use alcohol or drugs
  • Extreme mood swings or unpredictable behavior

As Abuse Escalates:

  • Intimidation and threats
  • Destroying property
  • Using children to control or manipulate
  • Economic control or financial abuse
  • Forced sex or sexual abuse
  • Physical violence of any kind

Remember: No one deserves to be abused. The abuse is NEVER your fault, regardless of what the abuser may say.

How to Help Someone Experiencing Abuse

Supporting someone who is experiencing abuse requires sensitivity, patience, and respect for their autonomy. Here's how you can help:

Dos:

  • Listen without judgment. Believe them.
  • Validate their feelings and experiences.
  • Let them know the abuse is not their fault.
  • Offer specific help ("Can I drive you to appointments?" instead of "Let me know if you need anything.")
  • Provide information about resources, but let them decide what to use.
  • Respect their decisions, even if you don't agree.
  • Continue to be supportive, regardless of their choices.
  • Help them create a safety plan if they're ready.

Don'ts:

  • Don't pressure them to leave if they're not ready.
  • Don't criticize or blame them for staying.
  • Don't speak negatively about the abuser (this can make them defensive).
  • Don't take control away from them by making decisions for them.
  • Don't give ultimatums or conditions for your support.
  • Don't put yourself in danger by directly confronting the abuser.

Remember: On average, it takes 7 attempts before someone leaves an abusive relationship permanently. Be patient and consistently supportive through this process.
